Intro

The PDE is:

∇2⋅u(t,x,y,z)=c2∂t2[u](t,z,y,z)\begin{aligned} \nabla^2 \cdot u(t, x, y, z) = c^2\partial_t^2 [u](t, z, y, z) \end{aligned} ∇2⋅u(t,x,y,z)=c2∂t2​[u](t,z,y,z)​

It models waves traveling in space.

Consider Cylindrical coordinate system:

[xyz]=[rcos⁡θrsin⁡θz]\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} r\cos\theta \\ r\sin\theta \\z \end{bmatrix} ⎣⎡​xyz​⎦⎤​=⎣⎡​rcosθrsinθz​⎦⎤​

And consider boundary conditions defined using a cylinder.

{u=0z=L,z=0u=0r=au(t,r,θ,z,)=u(t,r,θ+2π,z)∀θ,r,z∂θ[u](t,r,θ,z,)=∂θ[u](t,r,θ+2π,z)∀θ,r,z\begin{cases} u = 0 & z = L, z = 0 \\ u = 0 & r = a \\ u(t, r, \theta, z, ) = u(t, r, \theta + 2\pi, z) & \forall \theta, r, z \\ \partial_\theta[u](t, r, \theta, z, ) =\partial_\theta[u](t, r, \theta + 2\pi, z) & \forall \theta, r, z \end{cases} ⎩⎨⎧​u=0u=0u(t,r,θ,z,)=u(t,r,θ+2π,z)∂θ​[u](t,r,θ,z,)=∂θ​[u](t,r,θ+2π,z)​z=L,z=0r=a∀θ,r,z∀θ,r,z​

  1. The wave is bounded by the top and bottom of the cylinder.
  2. The wave is bounded by the body of the cylinder.
  3. The function inside the cylinder, the solution, has to be 2π2\pi2π periodic wrt to variable θ\thetaθ.

Consider substitution with cylindrical variable and separation of time and space domain, meaning that the solution takes the form:

u(t,r,θ,z)=T(t)ϕ(r,θ,z)T(t)∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)=c2T′′(t)ϕ(r,θ,z)T(t)c2T′′(t)=ϕ(r,θ,z)∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)=−λ2  ⟹  ϕ(r,θ,z)=−λ2∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)\begin{aligned} u(t, r, \theta, z) &= T(t)\phi(r, \theta, z) \\ T(t)\nabla^2 \cdot \phi(r, \theta, z) &= c^2 T''(t) \phi(r, \theta, z) \\ \frac{T(t)}{c^2T''(t)} &= \frac{\phi(r, \theta, z)}{\nabla^2\cdot\phi(r, \theta, z)} = -\lambda^2 \\\implies \phi(r, \theta, z) &= -\lambda^2 \nabla^2\cdot \phi(r, \theta, z) \end{aligned} u(t,r,θ,z)T(t)∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)c2T′′(t)T(t)​⟹ϕ(r,θ,z)​=T(t)ϕ(r,θ,z)=c2T′′(t)ϕ(r,θ,z)=∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)ϕ(r,θ,z)​=−λ2=−λ2∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)​

And that is the Helmholtz's equation.

ϕ(t,θ,z)=−λ2∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)(1)\phi(t, \theta, z) = -\lambda^2 \nabla^2\cdot \phi(r, \theta, z) \tag{1} ϕ(t,θ,z)=−λ2∇2⋅ϕ(r,θ,z)(1)

To solve that, we just use separation of variable. However, take notice that we are in the cylindrical coordinate, that means the Laplacian Operator will be:

∇2⋅∙=1r∂r[r∂r[∙]]+1r2∂θ2[∙]+∂z2[∙](2)\nabla^2\cdot \bullet = \frac{1}{r} \partial_r[r\partial_r[\bullet]] + \frac{1}{r^2}\partial_\theta^2[\bullet] + \partial_z^2[\bullet] \tag{2} ∇2⋅∙=r1​∂r​[r∂r​[∙]]+r21​∂θ2​[∙]+∂z2​[∙](2)

Bessel's Equation

The equation we are trying to solve is:

r∂r[r∂r[R]]+(r2(λ2−bl2)−m2)R=0(7)\begin{aligned} r\partial_r[r\partial_r[R]] + (r^2(\lambda^2 - b_l^2) - m^2)R &= 0 \end{aligned} \tag{7} r∂r​[r∂r​[R]]+(r2(λ2−bl2​)−m2)R​=0​(7)

But, this one is hard and that is why we will be solving another equation, and then use the solution for the other equation to solve this one.

The other equation we are solving instead is called the Bessel's Standard Form:

x∂[x∂x[y]]+(x2−m2)y=0(9)x\partial[x\partial_x[y]] + (x^2 - m^2)y = 0 \tag{9} x∂[x∂x​[y]]+(x2−m2)y=0(9)

where, yyy is a function of xxx. For more about actually solving this equation above using Frobenius method and all those cool shit, check out [[Bessel's Equation]] for a more detailed discussion.

I assume you read the part "Bessel's Non-standard form" for the file Bessel's Equation, if that is the case, then we know that the solution to expression (7) is in fact: Jm(λ2−bl2r)J_m(\sqrt{\lambda^2 - b_l^2}r)Jm​(λ2−bl2​​r)

Please recall that zm,nz_{m, n}zm,n​ is the nnn th roots for the Bessel's equation Jm(x)J_m(x)Jm​(x), for the Bessel's First kind.

Boundary conditions asserts:

Jm(λ2−bl2a)=0∧Jm(0)≠∞  ⟹  aλ2−bl2=zm,nλ2=zm,n2a2+bl2λl,m,n=zm,n2a2+bl2(10)\begin{aligned} J_m\left( \sqrt{\lambda^2 - b_l^2}a \right) = 0 \wedge J_m(0) &\neq \infty \\ \implies a\sqrt{\lambda^2 - b_l^2} &= z_{m, n} \\ \lambda^2 &= \frac{z_{m, n}^2}{a^2} + b_l^2 \\ \lambda_{l, m, n} &= \sqrt{\frac{z_{m, n}^2}{a^2}+ b_l^2} \end{aligned} \tag{10} Jm​(λ2−bl2​​a)=0∧Jm​(0)⟹aλ2−bl2​​λ2λl,m,n​​=∞=zm,n​=a2zm,n2​​+bl2​=a2zm,n2​​+bl2​​​(10)

We assme that the eigenvalues for the original PDE to be positive, from the separation of variables. This also makes sense [2]

Solution

Therefore, the solution of the Helmholtz equation is:

ϕ(θ,r,z)=∑m∈Z,n,l∈NRm,n(r)Θm(θ)Zl(z)\phi(\theta, r, z) = \sum_{m\in \mathbb{Z}, n, l \in \mathbb{N}}^{} R_{m, n}(r)\Theta_m(\theta)Z_l(z) ϕ(θ,r,z)=m∈Z,n,l∈N∑​Rm,n​(r)Θm​(θ)Zl​(z)

Where the Eigenfunction is like:

um,n,l(θ,r,z)=Jm(zm,n/a)Θm(θ)sin⁡(zlπL)u_{m, n, l}(\theta, r, z) = J_m(z_{m, n}/a)\Theta_m(\theta)\sin\left( \frac{zl\pi}{L} \right) um,n,l​(θ,r,z)=Jm​(zm,n​/a)Θm​(θ)sin(Lzlπ​)

Note The frequency of oscillation for waves in the cylinder is given as:

ωl,m,n=cλl,m,n=czm,na2+l2π2L2\omega_{l,m, n} = c\lambda_{l,m, n} = c\sqrt{\frac{z_{m, n}}{a^2} + \frac{l^2\pi^2}{L^2}} ωl,m,n​=cλl,m,n​=ca2zm,n​​+L2l2π2​​

And the unit is Radius Per minutes to get frequencies in Hz, please divides ωl,m,n\omega_{l,m, n}ωl,m,n​ by 2π2\pi2π. The Radius.
