Intro

Major Objective:

There is a connection between the QR decomposition on Krylov Subspace and the Arnoldi iterations that puts the matrix into upper Hessenberg form.

The Krylov subspace is spanning the same space as the QQQ matrix of the Hessenberg Similarity Transform, during the construction process of the QQQ matrix by the Arnoldi Iterations.

The Arnoldi Iterations assert this fact, and it can be consider the that Krylov Matrix written as the following:

Kn=[bAbAb2⋯An−1b]K_n = \begin{bmatrix} \\[1em] b & Ab & Ab^2 & \cdots A^{n - 1}b \\[1em] \\ \end{bmatrix} Kn​=⎣⎡​b​Ab​Ab2​⋯An−1b​⎦⎤​

Is spanned by the orthogonal subspace:

Kn=QnRnK_n = Q_nR_n Kn​=Qn​Rn​

The Krylov matrix is given by the product of the QQQ matrix from the Arnoldi Iterations and the RRR Matrix can be computed after we produced the QQQ matrix from the Arnoldi Iterations.

When Matters call for Krylov Subspace, we can use Arnoldi Iterations.

Warning:

Usually, KnK_nKn​ is very numerically unstable, and hence we only use the matrix in theoretical analysis, not actual implementation of numerical algorithm.

Observation:

Representing bbb using eigenvectors of AAA, and this KnK_nKn​ will tell use a lot about the Eigenvectors of the matrix AAA.

In Relations to Arnoldi Iterations

Claim 1

The matrix H~n\tilde{H}_{n}H~n​ from the Arnoldi iterations either by Modified GS, or the Householder Triangularization, will have the same eigenvector as the matrix AAA.

Claim 2

Using QR on Krylov Subspace can also directly give us the Hessenberg form of matrix AAA, which then makes the QnQ_nQn​ matrix from Anoldi Iterations the same as the QnQ_nQn​ matrix from applying QR factorization on Krylov Subspace of AAA. [1]

Consider:

Kn=[bAbA2b⋯An−1b]  ⟹  AKn=Kn+1[:,2:]=[AbA2bA3b⋯Anb]AKn=Kn[e2e3e4⋯enc](1)\begin{aligned} K_n &= \begin{bmatrix} b & Ab & A^2b & \cdots & A^{n - 1}b \end{bmatrix} \\ \implies AK_n &= K_{n + 1}[:, 2:] = \begin{bmatrix} Ab& A^2b & A^3b & \cdots & A^nb \end{bmatrix} \\ AK_n &= K_n \begin{bmatrix} e_2 & e_3 & e_4 \cdots e_n & c \end{bmatrix} \end{aligned}\tag{1} Kn​⟹AKn​AKn​​=[b​Ab​A2b​⋯​An−1b​]=Kn+1​[:,2:]=[Ab​A2b​A3b​⋯​Anb​]=Kn​[e2​​e3​​e4​⋯en​​c​]​(1)

Now, we make the assumption that KnK_nKn​ is invertable, so that means the matrix is full rank by my instinct. Then the following can be said:

(AKn)[:,−1]=KncAnb=Kncc=Kn−1Anb(2)\begin{aligned} (AK_n)[:, -1] &= K_nc \\ A^{n}b &= K_n c \\ c &= K_n^{-1}A^nb \end{aligned} \tag{2} (AKn​)[:,−1]Anbc​=Kn​c=Kn​c=Kn−1​Anb​(2)

Define:

Cn=[e2e3⋯enc](3)C_n = \begin{bmatrix} e_2 & e_3 & \cdots & e_n & c \end{bmatrix} \tag{3} Cn​=[e2​​e3​​⋯​en​​c​](3)

This is CnC_nCn​ famous and it's called the Companion Matrix

Claim 4 Without Proof

The Characteristic polynomial of the companion matrix CnC_nCn​, is ∣(Cn−λI)∣=0|(C_n - \lambda I)| = 0∣(Cn​−λI)∣=0 which is :

∣Cn−λI∣=zn+∑i=1ncizi−1 |C_n - \lambda I| = z^n + \sum_{i = 1}^{n} c_iz^{i - 1} ∣Cn​−λI∣=zn+i=1∑n​ci​zi−1

Where cic_ici​ is from the vector ccc.

continue from part (2) we have:

AKn=KnCnKn−1AKn=Cn(4)\begin{aligned} AK_n &= K_nC_n \\ K_n^{-1}AK_n &= C_n \end{aligned}\tag{4} AKn​Kn−1​AKn​​=Kn​Cn​=Cn​​(4)

Since AAA is similar to CnC_nCn​ they share the same eigenvalues, therefore, we can find the eigenvalues for matrix CnC_nCn​, then we find the eigenvalues for AAA. We are on our way to claim 1.

Consider applying QR factorization on KnK_nKn​, then we have the following argument:

Kn−1AKn=Cn(QR)−1AQnR=CnR−1QnHAQnR=CnQnHAQn=RCnR−1undefinedH~n(5)\begin{aligned} K_n^{-1}AK_n &= C_n \\ (QR)^{-1}AQ_nR &= C_n \\ R^{-1}Q_n^HAQ_nR &= C_n \\ Q_n^HAQ_n &= \underbrace{RC_nR^{-1}}_{\tilde{H}_n} \end{aligned}\tag{5} Kn−1​AKn​(QR)−1AQn​RR−1QnH​AQn​RQnH​AQn​​=Cn​=Cn​=Cn​=H~n​RCn​R−1​​​(5)

Observe CnR−1C_nR^{-1}Cn​R−1 is Hessenberg form, multiplying on RRR keeps the Hessenberg form. By definition, this is a hessenberg transform on matrix AAA. [2]

Therefore, H~n\tilde{H}_nH~n​ has the same eigenvalues as CnC_nCn​, therefore, H~n\tilde{H}_nH~n​ has the same eigenvectors as AAA.

Therefore, the H~n\tilde{H}_nH~n​, Hessenberg matrix for AAA has the same eigenvalue as the matrix AAA. Claim 1 is proven. ■\blacksquare■.

In part (5), the QnQ_nQn​ is from the QR factorization on KnK_nKn​, And it's the same unitary transformation that gives the Hessenberg, Claim 2 is proven ■\blacksquare■.

Another Proof for Claim 2

Different from the previous proof, this claim consider the recurrence of the orthogonal vectors without assuming that the Krylov Subspace of the matrix exists.

This proof is collected from Tyler Chen, a student who is in the same department as mine, link to his explanation.

Start by assuming strongly inductively that at the kkk iterations of the Arnoldi iterations, the following facts hold:

  • ⟨q1,q2,⋯ ,qk⟩=⟨b,Ab,⋯Ak−1b⟩\langle q_1, q_2, \cdots, q_k\rangle = \langle b, Ab, \cdots A^{k - 1}b\rangle⟨q1​,q2​,⋯,qk​⟩=⟨b,Ab,⋯Ak−1b⟩, the subspace span by the vectors from the orthogonalization is the same as the Krylov Subspace. They have the alternative notations of ⟨Qk⟩\langle Q_k\rangle⟨Qk​⟩ and ⟨Kk⟩\langle K_{k}\rangle⟨Kk​⟩, for all k≤nk \le nk≤n, where nnn is can be disputed depending on the rank of matrix AAA.

By the assumption that qkq_kqk​ from the Arnoldi Iteration is a vector in the subspace spanned by columns (By hypothesis of the inductive statement) of KkK_{k}Kk​, we have the expression that:

qk=c0+c1Ab+c2A2b+⋯+ck−1Ak−1bAqk=c0Ab+c1Ab2+c2A3b+⋯+ck−1AkbAqk=∑i=0k−2(ciAi+1b)undefined∈⟨Kk−1⟩+ck−1Akb(6)\begin{aligned} q_k &= c_0 + c_1 Ab + c_2 A^2 b + \cdots + c_{k - 1}A^{k - 1}b \\ Aq_k &= c_0Ab + c_1 Ab^2 + c_2 A^3 b + \cdots + c_{k - 1}A^{k}b \\ Aq_k &= \underbrace{\sum_{i = 0}^{k - 2}\left(c_{i} A^{i + 1}b\right)}_{\in \langle K_{k - 1}\rangle} + c_{k - 1}A^{k}b \end{aligned}\tag{6} qk​Aqk​Aqk​​=c0​+c1​Ab+c2​A2b+⋯+ck−1​Ak−1b=c0​Ab+c1​Ab2+c2​A3b+⋯+ck−1​Akb=∈⟨Kk−1​⟩i=0∑k−2​(ci​Ai+1b)​​+ck−1​Akb​(6)

The arnodi iterations will orthogonalize the vector AqkAq_kAqk​ against the subpsace ⟨Qk−1⟩\langle Q_{k - 1}\rangle⟨Qk−1​⟩, or ⟨Kk−1⟩\langle K_{k - 1}\rangle⟨Kk−1​⟩. Consider:

qk+1=proj⟨Qk⟩⊥(Aqk)=proj⟨Kk−1⟩⊥(Aqk)=proj⟨Kk−1⟩⊥(∑i=0k−2(ciAi+1b)undefined∈⟨Kk−1⟩+ck−1Akb)=proj⟨Kk−1⟩⊥(ckAkb)(7)\begin{aligned} q_{k + 1} &= \underset{\langle Q_k\rangle^\perp}{\text{proj}} (Aq_k) = \underset{\langle K_{k -1}\rangle^\perp}{\text{proj}}(Aq_k) \\ &= \underset{\langle K_{k -1}\rangle^\perp} {\text{proj}} \left( \underbrace{\sum_{i = 0}^{k - 2}\left(c_{i} A^{i + 1}b\right)}_{\in \langle K_{k - 1}\rangle} + c_{k - 1}A^{k}b \right) \\ &= \underset{\langle K_{k -1}\rangle^\perp}{\text{proj}} \left( c_kA^kb \right) \end{aligned}\tag{7} qk+1​​=⟨Qk​⟩⊥proj​(Aqk​)=⟨Kk−1​⟩⊥proj​(Aqk​)=⟨Kk−1​⟩⊥proj​⎝⎛​∈⟨Kk−1​⟩i=0∑k−2​(ci​Ai+1b)​​+ck−1​Akb⎠⎞​=⟨Kk−1​⟩⊥proj​(ck​Akb)​(7)

Notation

Here, the Proj\text{Proj}Proj operator projects the vector into the subspace noted by the subscript and then describe the vector under the standard basis.

The above statement says that equivalence between orthogonalizing AqkAq_kAqk​ onto the previous QR Subspace (QkQ_kQk​) and orthogonalizing AkbA^kbAkb onto the previous Krylov Subspace Kk−1K_{k - 1}Kk−1​.

The constant, ckc_kck​ will get lost after the normalization, producing qk+1q_{k + 1}qk+1​ that is the same from the Arnoldi Iterations.

Inductively, we have shown the the qk+1q_{k + 1}qk+1​ produced by orthogonalizing against the qjq_jqj​ from arnoldi iterations is the same as orthogonalizing against the vectors from the Krylov Subspace.

AQk=QkHk+hk+1qk+1ekT=QH~k\begin{aligned} AQ_k &= Q_kH_k + h_{k + 1}q_{k + 1}\mathbf{e}_k^T = Q\tilde{H}_k \end{aligned} AQk​​=Qk​Hk​+hk+1​qk+1​ekT​=QH~k​​
