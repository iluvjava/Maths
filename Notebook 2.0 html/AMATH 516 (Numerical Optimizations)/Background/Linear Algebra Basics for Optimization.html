Intro

We covers:

Inner Products and Linear maps.

List of important things:

  • Euclidean space E\mathbf{E}E, is a vector space defined together with the inner products over the vector space.

    • Some examples are like Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, Rm×n\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}Rm×n.

  • V\mathcal{V}V: denotes some kind of vector space over a given Euclidean space.

The idea of Inner products, linear maps a special kind of linear maps: Linear Operator arises from these.

Inner Products

Some of the examples for intuitive understanding:

⟨x::Rn,y::Rn⟩::R:=xTy⟨X::Rm×n,Y::Rm×n⟩::R:=Tr(XTY)\begin{aligned} & \left\langle x::\mathbb{R}^n, y::\mathbb{R}^n \right\rangle ::\mathbb{R} := x^Ty \\ & \left\langle X::\mathbb{R}^{m\times n} , Y::\mathbb{R}^{m\times n} \right\rangle ::\mathbb{R} := \text{Tr}(X^TY) \end{aligned} ​⟨x::Rn,y::Rn⟩::R:=xTy⟨X::Rm×n,Y::Rm×n⟩::R:=Tr(XTY)​

As we can see, matrices and vector are examples of Euclidean space and they have inner products fdefined over there. In the most general sense:

⟨::E,::E⟩:E×E↦R\left\langle ::\mathbf{E},::\mathbf{E} \right\rangle: \mathbf{E}\times \mathbf{E} \mapsto \mathbb{R} ⟨::E,::E⟩:E×E↦R

And the inner produce adhere to the following properties:

  • Symmetrical
  • Bilinear
  • Positive Definiteness

Linear Map

A liner mapping is a mapping craeted between 2 Euclidean spaces, say X,Y\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{Y}X,Y, to this regard, let's say we have a linear operator AAA defined as a mapping from X\mathbf{X}X to Y\mathbf{Y}Y.

Adjoint:

For any given linear mapping, mapping between 2 Euclidean spaces (could be the same, or different), there exists an adjoint operator, denoted as A∗A^*A∗ such that:

⟨Ax,y⟩=⟨x,A∗y⟩∀  x∈X,y∈Y\left\langle Ax, y \right\rangle = \left\langle x, A^*y \right\rangle \quad \forall \; x\in \mathbf{X}, y\in \mathbf{Y} ⟨Ax,y⟩=⟨x,A∗y⟩∀x∈X,y∈Y

And, A∗A^*A∗ maps from Y\mathbf{Y}Y to X\mathbf{X}X.

Linear Operator:

A linear operator maps from the same Euclidean space back to itself, if we are talking about Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn then it would be a square matrix over R\mathbb{R}R.

Self-Adjoing Linear Opertor

Symmetric or Hermitian matrices, AH=AA^H = AAH=A.

And then, we are back to the normal definition of P.S.D, and P.D matrices. [[Hermitian Adjoint]].

Monotone Operator

Came out of no where, this operator doesn't have to be linear too, but it's on euclidean spaces and has this properties:

⟨Tx−Ty,x−y⟩≥0∀x,y∈E\left\langle Tx - Ty, x - y \right\rangle \ge 0 \quad \forall x, y \in \mathbf{E} ⟨Tx−Ty,x−y⟩≥0∀x,y∈E

Norms

Make sure to read [[Norm]], which contains a lot of examples.

Norm maps from vector space to RRR.

∥⋅∥:V↦R\Vert \cdot \Vert : \mathcal{V} \mapsto \mathbb{R} ∥⋅∥:V↦R

Notice that E\mathbf{E}E, has relations with V\mathcal{V}V , in the way that, Euclidean space is a finite dimentional vector space over RRR.

Inner products defines some default norms: Euclidean Norms

∥x∥=⟨x,x⟩\begin{aligned} \Vert x\Vert = \sqrt{\left\langle x, x \right\rangle} \end{aligned} ∥x∥=⟨x,x⟩​​

And one of the property unique to the Euclidean norm with the same inner products that induced the norm is the Cauchy Inequality:

∣⟨x,y⟩∣≤∥x∥∥y∥∀x,y∈E|\left\langle x, y \right\rangle| \le \Vert x\Vert \Vert y\Vert \quad \forall x, y \in \mathbf{E} ∣⟨x,y⟩∣≤∥x∥∥y∥∀x,y∈E

Dual Norm

Definition:

∥v∥p=max⁡{⟨v,x⟩,∥x∥p≤1}\Vert v\Vert_p = \max\{\left\langle v, x \right\rangle, \Vert x\Vert_p\le 1\} ∥v∥p​=max{⟨v,x⟩,∥x∥p​≤1}

Take notice that, in the case of ppp norms over Real vector spaces, and we have 2 norm, p,qp, qp,q, if p−1+q−1=1p^{-1} + q^{-1} = 1p−1+q−1=1, then they are dual norm of each other.

Which implies that inf⁡,1\inf, 1inf,1 are dual to each other, and 222 norms are self-dual.

Generalized Cahcy-Schwarz Inequality

∣⟨x,y⟩∣≤∥x∥∥y∥∗|\left\langle x, y \right\rangle| \le \Vert x\Vert \Vert y\Vert^* ∣⟨x,y⟩∣≤∥x∥∥y∥∗

Now the Cauchy inequality can be defined over Non-euclidean norms, using dual norm.
