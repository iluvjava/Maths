Intro

Joint distribution. A joint distribution function is a function with 2 variables.

f(x,y)>0∀  x∈Ωx,y∈Ωyf(x, y) > 0 \quad \forall\; x \in \Omega_x, y \in \Omega_y f(x,y)>0∀x∈Ωx​,y∈Ωy​

And it's sum has to be positive, because it's a density function:

∬−∞∞f(x,y)dxdy=1\iint_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x, y) dxdy = 1 ∬−∞∞​f(x,y)dxdy=1

often, in the discrete case, the functions joint distribution function is written as:

pX,Y(x,y)p_{X, Y}(x, y) pX,Y​(x,y)

If, XXX and YYY are having their own independent probability distribution, say: pxp_xpx​, a univariate function and pyp_ypy​ also univariate, then their joint distribution is:

pX,Y=pX(x)pY(y)p_{X, Y} =p_X(x)p_Y(y) pX,Y​=pX​(x)pY​(y)

Just multiply them together and we will have it.

Fundamental Laws of Joint Probability

P(X=x∧Y=y)=P(X=x∣Y=y)P(Y=y)P(X=x∣Y=y)=P(X=x∧Y=y)P(Y=y)\begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}\left(X=x \wedge Y = y\right) &= \mathbb{P}\left(X = x|Y = y\right)\mathbb{P}\left(Y = y\right) \\ \mathbb{P}\left(X = x| Y = y\right) &= \frac{\mathbb{P}\left(X = x \wedge Y = y\right)} { \mathbb{P}\left(Y = y\right) } \end{aligned} P(X=x∧Y=y)P(X=x∣Y=y)​=P(X=x∣Y=y)P(Y=y)=P(Y=y)P(X=x∧Y=y)​​

Take these formula to understand the distribution and other kind of stuff.

pX∣Y(a∣b)=P(X=a∣Y=b)=pX,Y(a,b)py(b)=pY∣X(b∣a)pX(a)pY(b)p_{X|Y}(a|b) = \mathbb{P}\left(X = a | Y = b\right) = \frac{p_{X, Y}(a, b)}{p_y(b)} = \frac{p_{Y|X}(b|a)p_X(a)}{p_Y(b)} pX∣Y​(a∣b)=P(X=a∣Y=b)=py​(b)pX,Y​(a,b)​=pY​(b)pY∣X​(b∣a)pX​(a)​

Take note that, there is a slight difference in the subscript, and the equality is that:

pX,Y(a,b)=pX∣Y(b∣a)pX(a)p_{X, Y}(a, b) = p_{X|Y}(b|a)p_X(a) pX,Y​(a,b)=pX∣Y​(b∣a)pX​(a)

The joint distribution function of random variable X,YX, YX,Y, with inputs a,ba, ba,b will be the same as pX∣Yp_{X|Y}pX∣Y​, probability of X=bX = bX=b given Y=aY=aY=a, the prior, multiplied by pX(a)p_X(a)pX​(a), the probability of observing XXX becomes aaa.

Conditional Expectation

E[X∣Y=y]=∑x∈ΩXxP(X=x∣Y=y)=∑x∈ΩXxpX,Y(x∣y)\mathbb{E}\left[X\right|Y = y] = \sum_{x \in \Omega_X}^{}x \mathbb{P}\left(X = x|Y = y\right) = \sum_{x\in \Omega_X}^{}xp_{X, Y}(x|y) E[X∣Y=y]=x∈ΩX​∑​xP(X=x∣Y=y)=x∈ΩX​∑​xpX,Y​(x∣y)

The expected value of XXX given that we observed Y=yY = yY=y is it's just the summing up along the line where Y=yY = yY=y.

Notice that, it's just a simple tweak if we want to look for a transformed Expected value for the random variable XXX:

E[g(X)∣Y=y]=∑x∈ΩXxP(g(X)=x∣Y=y)=∑x∈ΩXg(x)pX,Y(x∣y)\mathbb{E}\left[g(X)\right|Y = y] = \sum_{x \in \Omega_X}^{}x \mathbb{P}\left(g(X) = x|Y = y\right) = \sum_{x\in \Omega_X}^{}g(x)p_{X, Y}(x|y) E[g(X)∣Y=y]=x∈ΩX​∑​xP(g(X)=x∣Y=y)=x∈ΩX​∑​g(x)pX,Y​(x∣y)

Law of Total Expectation

Using the conditional expectation to get the total expectation of the random variable.

E[g(X)]=∑yE[g(X)∣Y=y]pY(y)\mathbb{E}\left[g(X)\right] = \sum_{y}^{} \mathbb{E}\left[g(X)| Y = y\right]p_Y(y) E[g(X)]=y∑​E[g(X)∣Y=y]pY​(y)

For each condition that yyy is observed for rv yyy, we sum up the expectation when for random variable XXX, then we have the total expectation for the variable g(X)g(X)g(X).

Notice that this is just basic conditional probability, and it makes intuitive sense.

Proof:

We go from the right to the left:

∑y∈ΩYE[g(X)∣Y=y]pY(y)\sum_{y \in \Omega_Y}^{}\mathbb{E}\left[g(X)| Y = y\right]p_Y(y) y∈ΩY​∑​E[g(X)∣Y=y]pY​(y)

Using the definition of the conditional expectation for g(X)g(X)g(X) given YYY is observed to be yyy, we have:

∑y∈ΩY(∑x∈ΩXg(x)pX∣Y(x∣y))pY(y)\sum_{y \in \Omega_Y}^{} \left( \sum_{x\in \Omega_X}^{} g(x)p_{X|Y}(x|y) \right) p_Y(y) y∈ΩY​∑​(x∈ΩX​∑​g(x)pX∣Y​(x∣y))pY​(y)

Multiply it in and swap the sums. Notice that pY(y)p_{Y}(y)pY​(y) is the prior to the conditional PX∣Y(x∣y)P_{X|Y}(x|y)PX∣Y​(x∣y), therefore their product is the joint distribution function pX,Yp_{X, Y}pX,Y​

∑x∈Ωx∑y∈Ωyg(x)pX∣Y(x∣y)pY(y)\sum_{x\in \Omega_x}^{} \sum_{y\in \Omega_y}^{} g(x)p_{X|Y}(x|y)p_Y(y) x∈Ωx​∑​y∈Ωy​∑​g(x)pX∣Y​(x∣y)pY​(y)

∑x∈Ωx∑y∈Ωyg(x)pX,Y(x,y)=E[g(X)]\sum_{x\in \Omega_x}^{} \sum_{y\in \Omega_y}^{} g(x)p_{X, Y}(x, y) = \mathbb{E}\left[g(X)\right] x∈Ωx​∑​y∈Ωy​∑​g(x)pX,Y​(x,y)=E[g(X)]

And by definition, this is the expected value for the joint distribution, of the random variable g(X)g(X)g(X)

■\blacksquare■
