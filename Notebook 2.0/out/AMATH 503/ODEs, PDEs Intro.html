PDE

The function is a multivariable function. Usually, it's u(x,t)u(x, t)u(x,t), where xxx is t e space and ttt is the time. Space can be a vector, representing the 3D reality.

Let's remind ourselves about derivative and what it actually represents:

∂tu=lim⁡Δt→0u(x,t+Δt)−u(x,t)Δt\partial_t u = \lim_{\Delta t \rightarrow 0} \frac{u(x, t + \Delta t) - u(x, t)}{\Delta t} ∂t​u=Δt→0lim​Δtu(x,t+Δt)−u(x,t)​

And taking the derivative wrt to xxx is the same thing, that is just the partial derivative.

Example: The Heat Equation

∂tu=c2∂xu0<x<L0<t<∞\partial_t u = c^2\partial_xu \quad 0 < x < L \quad 0 < t < \infty ∂t​u=c2∂x​u0<x<L0<t<∞

The boundary conditions and the initial condition will govern the solutions of the PDEs (Most of the time).

This equation is also the diffusion equation as well.

When it's a function wrt xxx and ttt, both scalar, this can be used to model the heat on a rod.

Solving the PDE tells us: What is the evolution of the heat on the rod.

Assume that the rod is kept insulated except both ends where the heat is fixed to 0 degree celsius.

Assume that at the beginning the rod has 100 celcius of heat evenly distributed in on the whole rod. And the both end of the rod are set to 000 celsius.

Initial condition:

u(x,0)=100u(x, 0) = 100 u(x,0)=100

Boundary Conditions:

u(0,t)=0u(L,t)=0u(0, t) = 0 \quad u(L, t) = 0 u(0,t)=0u(L,t)=0

Note: Solution might not work when ttt goes to infinity.

It might seem like that there is some kind of discontinuity at the time moment t→0t\rightarrow 0t→0, but the truth is, there will be some kind of oscillation at the boundary right after when time begins to progress.
