[[Fourier Transform (PDE Flavor)]] One of the very important application of Fourier Transform is its application to Solving PDEs in the infinite domain.

Intro

We are going to solve the wave equation on the infinite domain using the method of Fourier Transform and D'Alambert's Method.

Yes, the only thing we changed is the boundary conditions, because now it's on infinite domain.

Suppose that that we are modeling the guitar string with:

{utt−c2uxx=0x∈Rlim⁡x→±∞u=0u(x,0)=f(x)ut(x,0)=0undefinedg(x)\begin{cases} u_{tt} - c^2u_{xx} = 0 & x\in \mathbb{R} \\ \lim_{x\rightarrow \pm \infty }u = 0 \\ u(x, 0) = f(x) & u_t(x, 0) = \underbrace{0}_{g(x)} \end{cases} ⎩⎨⎧​utt​−c2uxx​=0limx→±∞​u=0u(x,0)=f(x)​x∈Rut​(x,0)=g(x)0​​​

This is the wave equation on infinite domain.

Separating the Initial Conditions

Notice that, the PDE problem is linear, hence we can separate the problem into 2 parts. Let's separate the initial conditions into 2 parts by thinking of the following: u=u1+u2u = u_1 + u_2u=u1​+u2​

{u1(x,0)=f(x)∂t[u1](x,0)=0u2(x,0)=0∂t[u2](x,0)=g(x)\begin{cases} u_1(x, 0) = f(x) & \partial_t[u_1](x, 0) = 0 \\ u_2(x, 0) = 0 & \partial_t[u_2](x, 0) = g(x) \end{cases} {u1​(x,0)=f(x)u2​(x,0)=0​∂t​[u1​](x,0)=0∂t​[u2​](x,0)=g(x)​

This is in the general cases where, the initial conditions contains both the initial conditions and the derivative information, and in that case that is why we need the above separations for the general case.

Note:

f(x)f(x)f(x) must be: Absolutely Integrable. So that means that integral over the whole domain of the solution must be some finite number. And the boundary at infinite is still going to reach zero because the function we are integrating is absolutely integrable, and shifting it left and right by finite amount of ttt is not going to break Fourier Transform.

Back onto topic: Fourier Transform

Here is the list of procedures we use to get the solution for the PDE:

  1. Take the equation to the Fourier Domain so it's an ODE in term of ttt and www.
  2. Use the ut(x,0)=0u_t(x, 0) = 0ut​(x,0)=0 get the solution format, in the Fourier Domain.
  3. Use the u(x,0)=f(x)u(x, 0) = f(x)u(x,0)=f(x) to get the undetermined coefficients for the system, in the Fourier domain.
  4. Do the Inverse Fourier Transform to get the solution in the original spatial domain.

Questions: Why we didn't use the Boundary Conditions?

Because the initial condition helped us to satisfies that part, the function is absolutely integrable, so whatever Fourier Transformations that are involved for this PDEs, it's going to keep that part. And for a function that is absolutely integrable, it will have to have limit that approaches zero as it goes to infinity.
