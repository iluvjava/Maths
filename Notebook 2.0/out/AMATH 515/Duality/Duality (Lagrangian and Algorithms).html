Definition: Lagarangian

Starts with our primal problem:

min⁡x{g(x)+h(Ax−b)+cTx}\min_x \left\lbrace g(x) + h(Ax - b) + c^Tx \right\rbrace xmin​{g(x)+h(Ax−b)+cTx}

And then during the dualization process, we have the lagrangian:

min⁡xsup⁡z{g(x)+cTx+zT(Ax−b)−h∗(z)⏟Lagarangian: L(x,z)}\min_x \sup_z \left\lbrace \underbrace{g(x) + c^Tx + z^T(Ax - b) - h^*(z)}_{\text{Lagarangian: }\mathcal{L}(x, z)} \right\rbrace xmin​zsup​⎩⎪⎪⎨⎪⎪⎧​Lagarangian: L(x,z)g(x)+cTx+zT(Ax−b)−h∗(z)​​⎭⎪⎪⎬⎪⎪⎫​

  • Lagrangian is convex in xxx, concave in zzz, it's concave in zzz because of the h∗(z)h^*(z)h∗(z) located there. This is a saddle point of the system. This encodes both primal and dual problem.
  • xxx is the primal variable and zzz is the dual variable.

Using the idea here, the primal dual pair can be rephrased as the following:

Primal: sup⁡zL(x,z)Dual: inf⁡xL(x,z)\text{Primal: }\sup_z \mathcal{L}(x, z) \quad \text{Dual: } \inf_x \mathcal{L}(x, z) Primal: zsup​L(x,z)Dual: xinf​L(x,z)

They are objectives that are inside the optimization operator.

And the duality theory can be phrased using the min max max min:

inf⁡xsup⁡zL(x,z)≥sup⁡zinf⁡xL(x,z)\inf_x\sup_z \mathcal{L}(x, z) \ge \sup_z\inf_x \mathcal{L}(x, z) xinf​zsup​L(x,z)≥zsup​xinf​L(x,z)

Saddle Point of the Lagrangian

Assuming: Strong Duality Holds

Assuming that xˉ\bar{x}xˉ are solutions to the primal and zˉ\bar{z}zˉ are solutions to the dual.

Then:

[xˉ,zˉ][\bar{x}, \bar{z}] [xˉ,zˉ]

As a point is a stationary point of the Lagarangian, it's a saddle point for the system. This will mean that:

0∈∂xL(x,z)∣(xˉ,zˉ)∧0∈∂zL(x,z)∣(xˉ,zˉ)0\in \partial_x \left. \mathcal{L}(x, z) \right|_{(\bar{x}, \bar{z})} \wedge 0\in \partial_z \left. \mathcal{L}(x, z) \right|_{(\bar{x}, \bar{z})} 0∈∂x​L(x,z)∣(xˉ,zˉ)​∧0∈∂z​L(x,z)∣(xˉ,zˉ)​

Why is this significant?

It gives up 3 representation of the optimality conditions for the closed convex optimization problem. They come from the optimal conditions from the sub-differential of the function, and the use of Fenchel Equality (The conjugate swapping trick).

By using the representation of the optimality conditions, we will be able to connect the dual and the primal solutions, using the conditions for the sub-differential.

Example: Constrained Least Square

Consider the optimization problem:

min⁡Cx≤d{12∥Ax−b∥2}\min_{Cx \le d} \left\lbrace \frac{1}{2}\Vert Ax - b\Vert^2 \right\rbrace Cx≤dmin​{21​∥Ax−b∥2}

The Lagrangian of the problem will be denoted by:

L(x,y)=12∥Ax−b∥2+zT(Cx−d)−δ+(z)\mathcal{L}(x, y) = \frac{1}{2}\Vert Ax - b\Vert^2 + z^T(Cx - d) - \delta_+(z) L(x,y)=21​∥Ax−b∥2+zT(Cx−d)−δ+​(z)

Where the primal variables are xxx and zzz, notice the implicit constraints for the dual problem is z≥0z\ge 0z≥0

Now we consider taking the partial derivative wrt to the primal variable, which will give us:

∂xL(x,y)=AT(Ax−b)+CTz\partial_x \mathcal{L}(x, y) = A^T(Ax - b) + C^Tz ∂x​L(x,y)=AT(Ax−b)+CTz

Where, the indicator function drops out, and we skipped some fancy derivative stuff. We have equality here because it's smooth wrt to primal variable xxx, now consider:

∂yL(x,y)=Cx−d−∂δ+(z)\partial_y \mathcal{L}(x, y) = Cx - d - \partial \delta_+(z) ∂y​L(x,y)=Cx−d−∂δ+​(z)
