Shur's Decomposition

Course Notes

prereq: [[SVD]] When the matrix happens to be normal, then the Shur decomposition is the the same as the SVD decomposition for the normal matrix (possibly with different signs), not only that, it will also be the Eigen Decomposition of the matrix too, which is really cool. prereq: [[Matrices]] read about normal matrix.

Intro

The Schur decomposition is applicable to all square matrices even if when the matrix is defected.

A=QTQHA = QTQ^H A=QTQH

Where the matrix A is expressed using the unitary matrix QQQ and a Upper Triangular Matrix TTT

This is the KEY to Eigenvalue problems.

Proof

The proof is inductive.

Inductively:

Let the matrix AAA be an m×mm\times mm×m complex matrix.

Let the matrix UUU be a unitary matrix but with the first nnn element be the orthogonal eigenspace with egenvalue of λ\lambdaλ.

Augment the matrix UUU such that from column nnn to mmm, all of them are orthogonal and unitary.

Consider the following way of write the matrix A:

UHAUU^H A U UHAU

It's not hard to see that the it can be decomposed into:

UHAU=[λIB0C]U^H A U = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & C \end{bmatrix} UHAU=[λI0​BC​]

Where BBB and CCC are submarices and the matrix III is n×nn\times nn×n, relevant to the dimension of the eigenspace of the given vector.

For induction, assume that there also exists an Shcur decomposition for the submatrix CCC then, for example let's say that:

C=VTVHC = VTV^H C=VTVH

Then the UHAUU^HAUUHAU can be expressed as:

UHAU=[λIB0VTVH]U^H A U = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & VTV^H \end{bmatrix} UHAU=[λI0​BVTVH​]

Ok, so amazingly, I have a matrix AAA that can prof this inductive hypothesis. The matrix QQQ is going to be:

Q=U[I00V]Q = U \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V \end{bmatrix} Q=U[I0​0V​]

Consider:

A=U[λIB0VTVH]UHA = U\begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & VTV^H \end{bmatrix}U^H A=U[λI0​BVTVH​]UH

And then let's multiply left hand side by QHQ^HQH

QHA=[I00VH]UHU[λIB0VTVH]UHQ^HA = \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V^H \end{bmatrix}U^H U\begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & VTV^H \end{bmatrix}U^H QHA=[I0​0VH​]UHU[λI0​BVTVH​]UH

QHA=[I00VH][λIB0VTVH]UHQ^HA = \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V^H \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & VTV^H \end{bmatrix}U^H QHA=[I0​0VH​][λI0​BVTVH​]UH

Multiply the right hand side by the matrix QQQ then we have:

QHA=[I00VH][λIB0VTVH]UHU[I00V]Q^HA = \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V^H \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & VTV^H \end{bmatrix}U^H U \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V \end{bmatrix} QHA=[I0​0VH​][λI0​BVTVH​]UHU[I0​0V​]

QHA=[I00VH][λIB0VTVH][I00V]Q^HA = \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V^H \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & VTV^H \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V \end{bmatrix} QHA=[I0​0VH​][λI0​BVTVH​][I0​0V​]

And after multiplying these component into the matrix, we have:

QHAQ=[I00VH][λIB0VTVH][I00V]Q^HAQ = \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V^H \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & B \\ \mathbf{0} & VTV^H \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I & \mathbf{0}\\ \mathbf{0} & V \end{bmatrix} QHAQ=[I0​0VH​][λI0​BVTVH​][I0​0V​]

QHAQ=[λIBV0T]Q^HAQ = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda I & BV \\ \mathbf{0} & T \end{bmatrix} QHAQ=[λI0​BVT​]

Notice that the matrix is upper triangular.

Demonstrating the Base Case

For base case we are going to consider when the matrix is 2×22 \times 22×2, it's not hard to prove it, and the V matrix is literally just a scalar, and that will automatically make the decomposed 2×22 \times 22×2 a upper triangular matrix.

Corollary: Spectral Theorem

When the matrix AAA is Hermitian, the the matrix TTT in the middle will become a diagonal matrix, hence Hermitian are Diagonalizable . This is the case because let's say that:

A=QHTQA = Q^H TQ A=QHTQ

And AAA is Hermitian so that AH=AA^H = AAH=A, then applying the Hermitian to the 3 matrices we will have:

AH=QHTHQ=A=QHTQA^H = Q^H T^HQ = A = Q^HTQ AH=QHTHQ=A=QHTQ

Which is saying that TH=TT^H = TTH=T.

Schur's decomposition assures that the TTT is upper-triangular, therefore, it has to be the case that TTT is diagonal.

Therefore, for all Hermitian matrix are diagonalizable because they are similar to a diagonal matrix.
