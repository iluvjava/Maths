Intro

When a trained model tries to predict something, there are 2 type of errors that arises.

  1. Error due the variance.
  2. Error due to bias.

Errors of these 2 types are connected in some ways.

Errors Due to Biases

Assume that, a lot of models are trained using different samples draw from the populations. (Ideally, infinitely many such models are trained.)

Then, the difference between the expected value of all the models and the actual ground truth expectation of the data.

Errors Due to Variance

The variability of the prediction made by all different kind of models we trained.

Mathematical Definition

Assume an underlying generative model of the form:

Y=f(X)+ϵY = f(X) + \epsilon Y=f(X)+ϵ

Where ϵ\epsilonϵ is some kind of noise with some kind of distribution that has a variance of σϵ\sigma_\epsilonσϵ​ and a mean of 000.

And let the model f^(x)\hat{f}(x)f^​(x) be our estimate of the model using observation.

Err(x)=E[(Y−f^(x))2]=E[(Y−f(x))2]+E[(f(x)−f^(x))2]\text{Err}(x) = \mathbb{E}\left[(Y - \hat{f}(x))^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[(Y - f(x))^2\right] + \mathbb{E}\left[(f(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2\right] Err(x)=E[(Y−f^​(x))2]=E[(Y−f(x))2]+E[(f(x)−f^​(x))2]

And this is the components of the learning errors of a trained model.

Proof

We are going to use some algebra tricks for this one and it's like:

E[(Y+f(x)−f(x)−f^(x))2]\mathbb{E}\left[ (Y + f(x) - f(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2 \right] E[(Y+f(x)−f(x)−f^​(x))2]

Which we then use the quadratic equation to get the expression that:

E[(Y−f(x))2+2(Y−f(x))(f(x)−f^(x))+(f(x)−f^(x))2]\mathbb{E}\left[ (Y - f(x))^2 + 2(Y - f(x))(f(x) - \hat{f}(x)) + (f(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2 \right] E[(Y−f(x))2+2(Y−f(x))(f(x)−f^​(x))+(f(x)−f^​(x))2]

We assume that the random variable f^(x)\hat{f}(x)f^​(x) is independent of the noise ϵ\epsilonϵ, then we can say that the cross term looks like:

E[2(Y−f(x))(f(x)−f^(x))]=2E[(Y−f(x))⏟ϵ]E[(f(x)−f^(x))]=0\mathbb{E}\left[ 2(Y - f(x))(f(x) - \hat{f}(x)) \right] = 2 \mathbb{E}\left[ \underbrace{ (Y - f(x))}_{\epsilon } \right] \mathbb{E}\left[ (f(x) - \hat{f}(x)) \right] = 0 E[2(Y−f(x))(f(x)−f^​(x))]=2E[ϵ(Y−f(x))​​]E[(f(x)−f^​(x))]=0

This part is by the independence. and the fact that the expected value of ϵ\epsilonϵ is zero, we know that the expected value of the cross term in fact zero.

Hence, the bias and variance trade off theorem can be ultimately written like:

Err(x)=E[(Y−f(x))2]⏟(1):Irreducible Error+E[(f(x)−f^(x))2]⏟(2): Learning Error\text{Err}(x) = \underbrace{ \mathbb{E}\left[(Y - f(x))^2\right]}_{(1): \text{Irreducible Error}} + \underbrace{ \mathbb{E}\left[(f(x) - \hat{f}(x))^2\right]}_{\text{(2): Learning Error}} Err(x)=(1):Irreducible ErrorE[(Y−f(x))2]​​+(2): Learning ErrorE[(f(x)−f^​(x))2]​​

  1. Irreducible error, and this is independent of the random variable f^(x)\hat{f}(x)f^​(x), which is the model we trained using some given samples.
  2. And the second term is the Learning Error. This can be reduce by make a lot of observations, and it's the error we will be working on.

Looking at the Learning Error more closely

Learning Error=E[(f(x)−E[f^(x)]+E[f^(x)]−f^(x))2]\text{Learning Error} = \mathbb{E}\left[ \left( f(x) - \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] + \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] - \hat{f}(x) \right)^2 \right] Learning Error=E[(f(x)−E[f^​(x)]+E[f^​(x)]−f^​(x))2]

Just expanding the inside we have the expression that:

(f(x)−E[f^(x)])2+2(f(x)−E[f^(x)])⏟ϵ(E[f^(x)]−f^(x))+(E[f^(x)]−f^(x))2\left( f(x) - \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] \right)^2 + 2\underbrace{ \left( f(x) - \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] \right)}_{\epsilon} \left( \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] - \hat{f}(x) \right) + \left( \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] - \hat{f}(x) \right)^2 (f(x)−E[f^​(x)])2+2ϵ(f(x)−E[f^​(x)])​​(E[f^​(x)]−f^​(x))+(E[f^​(x)]−f^​(x))2

Using the assumption that the noise is independence of the variable model f^(x)\hat{f}(x)f^​(x) produced, the cross term in the middle has expectation of zero.

Now we are left with 2 terms and this is like:

Learning Error=E[(f(x)−E[f^(x)])2]⏟(3):Bias2+E[(E[f^(x)]−f^(x))2]⏟(4):Variance\text{Learning Error} = \underbrace{ \mathbb{E}\left[ \left( f(x) - \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] \right)^2 \right]}_{(3):\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{ \mathbb{E}\left[ \left( \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] - \hat{f}(x) \right)^2 \right]}_{(4):\text{Variance}} Learning Error=(3):Bias2E[(f(x)−E[f^​(x)])2]​​+(4):VarianceE[(E[f^​(x)]−f^​(x))2]​​

And take note that, the learning errors consist of 2 pars too:

  1. This is the Bias, like, it's possible that our model could be off for some reasons, due to the limitation of our model.
  2. This is the Variance. This is he variance of the models we produce. Some of the learning errors created could just be the variance of the model. To overcome this. This is the term that govern the realm of over-fittings.

THIS, is the variance and the bias that we are going to be focus on for training machine learning models.

Bias2=(f(x)−E[f^(x)])2\text{Bias}^2 = \left( f(x) - \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right] \right)^2 Bias2=(f(x)−E[f^​(x)])2

Why the proof feels so weird?

Because we are making something out of nothing, the induction of the terms: f(x),E[f^(x)]f(x), \mathbb{E}\left[\hat{f}(x)\right]f(x),E[f^​(x)] is just a matter of intuitive understanding of the matter instead of something that is based on theorem and axioms.

Model Complexity

The variance increases with the complexity of the model and the biases will increase as the models get too simple.
